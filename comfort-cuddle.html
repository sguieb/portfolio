ComfortCuddle

A senior-focused health companion system: a plush bear, smart watch, and companion app, designed to help elderly individuals with memory loss live more independently.

Overview

ComfortCuddle is a speculative design project created for University of Washington's INFO 200: Intellectual Foundations of Informatics. Our team was tasked with imagining a future technology product that addresses a real social need, so we focused on the growing challenge of elder care for individuals with Alzheimer's and dementia.

The product consists of three components working together: a plush stuffed bear companion that serves as a physical presence and display device, a health monitoring smartwatch that tracks vitals and location, and a companion mobile app that connects the user to their family and healthcare providers.

My role covered the UX and app design portions of the project. I contributed to the low-fidelity wireframes, helped define the design principles, and designed the high-fidelity Figma prototype.

The Problem

16M+

Americans provide unpaid care for individuals with Alzheimer's or dementia.

5.8M

Americans are currently living with Alzheimer's, with millions more worldwide.

24/7

Caregiving demands that current solutions fail to address for those living alone.

As the aging population grows, many elderly individuals, particularly those with memory loss, struggle to maintain independence while staying safe and connected. Human caregivers face limits in availability, and existing tools like fitness trackers aren't designed with this audience in mind. ComfortCuddle was conceived to fill that gap: a product built specifically for individuals with dementia or Alzheimer's that prioritizes both dignity and safety.

Core Features

  1. 01

    Wellness Monitoring

    The companion smartwatch tracks heart rate, blood pressure, temperature, sleep patterns, and step count throughout the day. If major fluctuations are detected, the system automatically alerts the user's family members and healthcare provider. Medical alerts are sent if the user appears to be in danger, critical for individuals living alone.

  2. 02

    Health & Fitness

    The bear companion presents AI-customized meal plans and guides the user through cooking safely. The watch tracks location and workout duration, feeding data to an AI that generates personalized, adaptive exercise routines based on each user's activity levels and health status.

  3. 03

    Memory & Connection

    The app features a multimedia display that plays stored photos and videos, a memory recall feature that replays recorded messages from family members, and daily reminder notifications for medication, meals, and exercise. These features combat the isolation common in dementia patients by keeping them connected to loved ones and their own past.

Design Process

  1. 01

    Persona development

    We built a primary persona around Elizabeth Thompson, 75, a woman living alone with Alzheimer's whose family is unable to provide daily care. This grounded our design decisions in a specific, realistic user scenario rather than a generic elderly archetype.

  2. 02

    Low-fidelity wireframes

    We sketched the app's core screens including login, language selection, home dashboard, health tracking, memories, and wellness, mapping out navigation flows and identifying key content priorities for each screen before moving to digital tools.

  3. 03

    Design principles

    The final prototype was guided by five principles: a light theme for clarity, intuitive navigation with minimal steps, Face ID for frictionless login, full transparency around data collection, and deep personalization. We also prioritized voice assistance and integration with existing health record systems.

  4. 04

    High-fidelity prototype

    The Figma prototype covered onboarding, daily dashboard, medication reminders, audio messages from family, health data entry, and the memory gallery — all styled for readability and ease of use by users who may struggle with dense interfaces.

Reflection

This project was my first experience designing for a vulnerable user group, and it fundamentally changed how I think about accessibility. Designing for the elderly, especially those with cognitive impairment, means every extra tap, every dense block of text, and every ambiguous icon has real consequences. It pushed me to interrogate design choices I might otherwise take for granted.

If I were to revisit this project, I'd want to conduct actual user testing with elderly participants, incorporate caregiver feedback more directly, and research the ethical implications of health data collection more thoroughly. The privacy and consent considerations for this population are significant and deserve more than a single onboarding screen.
