Discover RSOs

A mobile app concept to help UW students quickly discover registered student organizations that match their interests — with quiz-based matching, advanced filtering, and a live event calendar.

Figma UX Research Wireframing Prototyping Usability Testing
Figma prototype ↗ Slides ↗

Overview

Discover RSOs is a mobile application concept designed to solve a real friction point at the University of Washington: students struggle to find clubs that fit their interests, and RSO leaders struggle to reach students beyond their existing social circles. The current platform, Huskylink, lacks the filtering, discoverability, and engagement features that both groups need.

Our team of five conducted user research with both RSO leaders and students, synthesized findings into personas and a problem statement, and iterated from low-fidelity wireframes through a fully tested high-fidelity Figma prototype. I contributed across research, wireframing, and the final prototype iteration following usability testing.

Research

  1. 01

    Domain expert interviews — RSO leaders

    We interviewed three RSO officers: a social media manager for Figure Skating at UW, the vice president of Hui Hoaloha ʻUlana, and the president of Hui Hoaloha ʻUlama. All three described the same core problem: Huskylink's algorithmic limitations made new member recruitment nearly impossible, forcing clubs to rely entirely on tabling events and word-of-mouth. Managing multiple social media platforms to compensate was time-consuming and still failed to reach students outside existing networks.

  2. 02

    Student survey

    An online survey of UW undergraduates found that 80% expressed need for an updated platform to better access RSOs. Students consistently asked for interest-based filtering, event calendars, direct officer contact, linked social media pages, and peer reviews — features entirely absent from the current Huskylink experience.

  3. 03

    Personas

    We synthesized research into two personas: Alex Anderson, 20, a club president who wants to grow her climbing club beyond her immediate friend group; and Bobbi Brown, 19, a psychology sophomore who feels disconnected from campus opportunities because he keeps missing events due to Huskylink's poor information architecture. These personas kept our design decisions anchored to real needs rather than assumptions.

Key Features

  1. 01

    Interest quiz

    A three-page questionnaire asks students about their free-time activities, preferred group environment, and skills they want to develop. The app then surfaces a matched list of RSOs — giving students who don't know where to start a structured, personalized entry point into the discovery process.

  2. 02

    Filtering

    For students with a clearer sense of what they want, the filter system allows browsing by interest category, time commitment (daily through monthly), club size, and whether an application is required. A reset button and "Apply" trigger keep the interface clean and reversible.

  3. 03

    Event calendar

    A persistent calendar in the main navigation shows all upcoming RSO events across the university. Color-coded bubbles on each date indicate events scheduled that day; tapping a date reveals event details with direct links to the hosting club's profile page and an Attend button.

Usability Testing & Iteration

We tested five UW students on specific tasks: joining an RSO, finding club information, accessing the event calendar, contacting a club officer, and completing event registration. Most features tested well intuitively. Three areas needed iteration: the "Attend" button was hard to find and moved to a more prominent position in the revised design; the calendar had too many navigation paths and was simplified; and the search page gained a "Take Quiz" entry point for students who weren't sure where to start.

For the club profile page, we renamed "Add club" to "Add" and "Learn more" to "About" to reduce button label ambiguity, and moved the "See All Events" link into the Upcoming Events section for more logical information architecture.

Reflection

This was my most complete end-to-end UX process to date — from research and synthesis through wireframing, prototyping, and usability testing. The biggest lesson was how much the research phase shaped the design. Several features I might have assumed were obvious (like a quiz entry point) only emerged because we heard directly from students that they felt lost without a structured starting point.

The limitations we identified — social media integration for RSO leaders, real-time event notifications, and full accessibility support — represent a clear roadmap for a future iteration. The accessibility gap in particular is something I'd want to address first: designing an RSO discovery tool that can't be used by screen reader users or those needing voice navigation undermines the inclusivity the app is meant to promote.
